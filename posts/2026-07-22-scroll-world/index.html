Scroll worlds: one engine, three flights

I found oso95/scroll-world, a skill that turns a brand into a scroll-scrubbed camera flight through AI-generated video. The idea is great and the video pipeline is the expensive part: image gens, video gens, frame-locked seams, credits. So I rebuilt the flow in real time. Scroll still drives a continuous camera through a chain of scenes, but the scenes render live in three.js, which means no asset generation, no credit spend, and no seams, because one continuous camera has nothing to stitch.

Three builds came out of the same engine in one session. The flagship is a Google Earth style dive through the ten biggest cities on the planet.

You start about 14,000 km out over a NASA Blue Marble sphere. The planet rotates to Japan, the camera dives to 27 km over Tokyo, a real Esri satellite patch fades in on the surface at its true 19.6 km footprint, a card shows the 2026 population, and then you climb back to orbit and the planet turns to Delhi. Ten dives, ranked by World Population Review 2026 agglomeration numbers, with a running counter that ends at the exact 263.0M sum of all ten.

Same engine, different scene content. This one flies the Khumbu valley from Lukla to Everest over real SRTM elevation at true vertical scale, textured with 320 stitched Esri tiles, with expedition and death stats per peak from the Himalayan Database.

The invariant frame

Every build is the same five pieces, and only the scene content changes.

  1. A tall scroll track over a fixed full-viewport canvas.
  2. One camera path, a CatmullRomCurve3 through hand-placed waypoints, sampled at the scroll fraction.
  3. A smoothed camera and a raw UI. The camera follows the scroll target with a lerp for weight; every card, dot, and counter reads the raw scroll fraction so the UI is correct at any frame rate.
  4. Per-scene copy cards with real stats, and rail dots that jump between scenes.
  5. A loader counting inlined assets, a scroll hint, and a footer citing every source.

Data and imagery, all keyless

elevation  s3.amazonaws.com/elevation-tiles-prod/terrarium/{z}/{x}/{y}.png
imagery    server.arcgisonline.com/.../World_Imagery/MapServer/tile/{z}/{y}/{x}
globe      eoimages.gsfc.nasa.gov/.../world.topo.bathy.200412.3x5400x2700.jpg

Terrarium elevation decodes as R*256 + G + B/256 - 32768 meters. Everything gets stitched, resized, jpg-encoded, and inlined as base64, so each page is one file with no runtime fetches beyond the three.js CDN script tag. Base64 adds 37% to raw bytes; each of these pages lands between 4 and 6 MB.

GOTCHA · LIGHTING
three r155+ switched to physically correct lighting, so a Lambert material under an ambient light renders satellite imagery dark. Sunlight is already baked into the photos. Use MeshBasicMaterial for anything textured with real imagery.
GOTCHA · HEADLESS QA
Headless chromium runs software WebGL at a few fps, so the smoothed camera lags scroll and early screenshots look broken when nothing is wrong. Assert UI state right after scrolling, since it reads the raw fraction, and judge framing by center-region pixel mean and std after a 15 to 30 second settle instead of by eye.

The skill

The whole flow is written up as a generic agent skill in SKILL.md next to this post: the invariant frame, the scene-content menu from built geometry to globe dives, the keyless endpoints, the engine rules, and the headless verification method. Point an agent at it with any subject and a ranked list of places or stats and it should come back with a working flight.
